Results and Discussion¶

We use a confusion matrix to provide a two by two matrix providing four outputs of True Positive, False Negative, False Positive, and True Negative. The accuracy is calculated by (True Positive + True Negative) / Total. Precision is calculated by (True Positive) / (True Positive + False Positive), it means how good the prediction is when prediction is positive. We also use adjusted mutual information score (AMIS), which accounts for chance, measuring the similarity between assignments regardless of permutation.

With the data, we used five different methods: K-means clustering, decision tree, random forest, logistic regression, and MLP. We measure the accuracy and precision of the confusion mixture as well as the adjusted mutual information score for each result.

First method we use is unsupervised learning, the K-means clustering, which divides our data into two clusters. The accuracy of the confusion matrix between prediction and true value of our data, is 0.4940374787052811, which means nearly half of our prediction is correct, the rest incorrect. The precision is 0.45600756859035, which is also a close value to 0.5, meaning that the prediction is positive about half of the time.

The second and the third method we use are the decision tree and random forest, both supervised learning. They render very close adjusted mutual information score, accuracy and precision. This possibly means that our prediction would achieve similar accuracy, precision, and adjusted mutual information regardless of how far away the prediction is, which is expected, because there should not be much difference between further and close prediction

The fourth method and fifth method, logistic regression and MLP, both supervised learning, also achieve similar accuracy and precision approaching 0.5. Though MLP achieve a relatively higher adjusted mutual information score than the rest of the methods, the value is still very close to zero, which means the overall adjusted mutual information score for all methods are still approaching zero.

Overall, the results, no matter what algorithm and evaluation metric used, were not promising as to predicting a persons chance of recovering from complications from the COVID-19 vaccine. Hence, the results are inconclusive as to whether age, number of vaccine doses, and sex are good predictors of a persons chance of recovering from those complications. However, the most promising model was MLP since it consistently had the highest accuracy as well as one of the best precisions of all the models tried.
