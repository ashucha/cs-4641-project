Introduction

Though COVID-19 vaccine has proven its efficiency, many still remain hesitant due to potential side effects. Side effects include the majority reporting mild to moderate symptoms, such as fever and fatigue, but these symptoms generally would not cause long-term conditions, though rare cases of severe symptoms or potential death are possible. We will use machine learning to build a model that helps people make a decision of whether to take the vaccine or not by predicting possible outcomes/syndromes of a person taking the vaccine given indicating factors, such as age and gender.

Problem Definition

There are a couple of reasons why we want to tackle the challenge of vaccine hesitancy through this project. The first is that a large enough portion of the population is hesitant to get the vaccine because herd immunity is far from achievable at the current level. Specifically, only about 83% of Americans have received at least one dose while only 71% is fully vaccinated. According to the Mayo Clinic, “94% of the population must be immune to interrupt the chain of transmission” of COVID-19, a highly optimistic goal at this rate. The second reason why we chose this issue is due to new developments in COVID-19 and the fight against it. This includes the fact that the FDA is looking into authorizing the vaccine for children under the age of 5, a fourth dose expected to be available soon, and COVID cases recently rising. These developments give lots of opportunities for misinformation to expand beyond what has already been spread.

Method

The potential methods that we may use include K-means clustering or hierarchical clusterings based on the number of data and the number of factors of data. According to the data we have already got, the factors include age, sex, weight and so on. Since the factors are all discrete variables, thus classification may be another method that we will consider.

Data

We have already found some data online that we would analyze. The one dataset is from the FDA (US Food and Drug Administration) and CDC (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention), and the other one is from New York Times. We will try to get more official and more reliable data from more official websites. There are two datasets that we have downloaded so far: worldwide percentage of population vaccinated and worldwide adverse reactions after vaccination. By using clustering methods, we will take deeper analysis on dividing them into several groups based on the factors of ages, gender, symptoms after vaccination, times for vaccination, and etc.

Potential Results and Discussion

We currently plan to use the adjusted_rand_score metric from sklearn to evaluate our clustering performance. This metric compares the predicted clusters with a defined ground truth to output the accuracy. Additionally, we expect to find that patient demographics do correlate with the severity of vaccine-related side effects. At this moment, we don't know which specific features will correlate better with the side effects, but that is the reason we are undertaking this project.
